For most of my adult life, I worked with things you can measure. Data. Models. Dashboards. The clean grammar of proof. I'm not the kind of person who cries at lines on a page.

Except I do.

Not in public. But sometimes a poem finds the exact nerve no dataset ever mapped, and something releases. I didn't plan for that. I don't have a framework for it. And I've stopped pretending the two lives are separate.

Poetry is supposed to stay in its lane. Literature class. Soft-cover journals. The corner of life designated for feelings you can't organize.

It doesn't stay there. It shows up in a line of code you wrote at 2 AM that reads like confession. In the way your mother once described rain. In the grief that arrived when a number on a screen told you something true you weren't ready for.

The lyric form is the one that admits what human experience refuses to sort. That's not a romantic claim. That's just accurate.

Here's the thing about elegant code: programmers call it *beautiful*. They always have. There's a reason for that.

a specific moment — a commit message, a recursive function, a line of output that felt like a volta — something real and particular

Some weeks I build dashboards, review model outputs, stare at analytics. Other weeks I write poems nobody may ever read. For years I kept those as separate identities. Then I noticed the same thing drove both — the desire to understand what people actually feel beneath what they say.

One used numbers. The other used metaphor. Tools different. Curiosity identical.

The best scientific papers have rhythm. The best commit messages have grief in them sometimes. Poetry was already in the server room. It was never waiting for an invitation.

The toast burns. You scrape it over the sink. That smell — charred edges, still-warm bread underneath. You've done this a hundred times.

But one morning a poem lives inside it. Not one you write. One you *inhabit*, briefly, before the day takes over.

Poetry is not elevated experience. It's ordinary experience, seen clearly for one second. The grey of a commute counts. The brown of bad coffee counts. The particular blue of a Tuesday afternoon when nothing happens and you feel, inexplicably, okay.

You don't have to write any of this down. Notice it didn't come from somewhere special. Notice it came from Tuesday.

No one sits down to feel grief poetically. It arrives unannounced, in the wrong font.

But grief has always been poetry's country. Not because poets romanticize loss — the best ones don't. Because grief insists on *specificity*.

👏 CLAP

You don't miss a person. You miss how they said your name. The exact pitch of their laugh at something stupid. There are losses I can summarize in a sentence. Others I can only approach through poems.

Facts tell you what happened. Poetry tells you what stayed behind after it happened.

That's not a small mercy. It's the reason people reach for poems at funerals and not spreadsheets.

The times poetry has landed hardest on me, I wasn't looking for it. I was tired. Half-reading. Distracted by something smaller.

That's the trap in waiting for elevated moments. Poetry comes sideways. In a lyric you've heard forty times that suddenly means something different. In a sentence someone says to you that you'll carry for years without knowing why.

It dips its fingers in every colour because it's patient. It waits in the ordinary and the technical and the unbearable until you're open — just barely — and then it reaches you.

The door doesn't need to be wide. A crack is enough.

There's no lesson here. That's the point.

Poetry doesn't sharpen your frameworks. It doesn't solve problems. It makes you a person who has stood inside a colour, briefly, and recognized it for what it was.

That is not a small thing. It is the whole thing.

The poem finds you. Your only job is to not keep the door entirely shut.
