---

There's a moment in loving someone when you stop protecting them from your life and start wanting to weave them into it. Not gradually. All at once. You're in the dark, wrapped in a blanket that feels like the whole world, and you think: I want everyone I love to see what I see when I look at you. Impossible and inevitable at once. That's when you stop asking if they're good enough and start asking if you're brave enough to let them in that deep.

---

Safety alone is incomplete. So is aliveness without safety. Most love songs collapse into one or the other — safe becomes settling, alive becomes chaos. This poem refuses to choose. The person you build a life with isn't the one who makes you smaller or more careful. They hold you steady while you expand. Your nervous system relaxes. Your aliveness opens up. Both things, at the same time, neither cancelling the other out.

And it's not abstract here. The poem gives you the texture: darkness, blanket, bonfire, mama. These aren't symbols. They're the actual places where you stop performing and just exist. Where vulnerability stops being a risk and becomes a shared language. You're not managing an impression of yourself. You're just there. That's what "safe but alive" actually means.

---

But here's what the poem really catches — and what most love stories get wrong. It's not about what the beloved does *for* you. It's about what they do to you when you *witness* them being loved by someone else. When you imagine them with your people.

The speaker doesn't melt because they're adored. They melt because their beloved lights up talking about someone else. That's a different ache entirely.

It's the ache of wanting integration. Of wanting the circles of your life to collapse into one. Your mama looks at them and recognizes something worth loving. They sit in the darkness and the warmth and they actually *belong* there. Not as a guest. As someone who was always supposed to be there.

[PAUSE]

That's not the fantasy of being chosen. It's the fantasy of being chosen by someone your whole world would also choose.

[BROLL: older woman laughing with a younger woman at a kitchen table, warm evening interior, genuine ease between them]

---

The poem ends with melting. "Dissolving in my arms / out of sheer love and embarrassment." Not triumph. Not arrival. Dissolution.

The embarrassment is the key. It's not the embarrassment of being caught loving someone. It's the vulnerability of wanting something so badly you can already feel its texture. Already know what it tastes like. Already know you'd risk everything for it.

This isn't a memory. It's a dream that's almost real enough to touch. The speaker's holding a future in their hands — so specific, so warm — that the holding of it is already undoing them.

[PAUSE]

When someone you love becomes real in the presence of your family, and you get to see them at rest in a circle that includes you both, something in you comes loose. Not apart. Loose. Like a knot finally giving way.

[BROLL: close-up, two people side by side in candlelight, one resting their head on the other's shoulder, soft and still]

---

Love isn't complete until you can imagine the person in your life being fully alive in the lives of the people you came from. Safe doesn't mean unchanged. Alive doesn't mean reckless. They live in the same moment — the moment when someone sees you entire and decides to show up as their entire self too. When that happens, you stop being divided. You stop needing permission from different parts of your life to love someone.

You just love them.

[PAUSE]
