There's a specific kind of madness that doesn't announce itself. One day you're fine. The next, someone's laugh is living rent-free in your head, your morning coffee tastes different, and the part of your brain that's supposed to run the show has quietly handed over the keys.

The trouble isn't the feeling. It's what comes after — when you realize you've been running on a hangover you didn't consent to. And it refuses to lift.

The hangover metaphor works because hangovers have an endpoint. You sleep it off, you hydrate, you move on.

This one doesn't schedule a checkout time.

And what keeps it alive isn't a vague ache — it's detail. Twinkling eyes. An enlightening smile. Ebony hair. The poem doesn't soften into abstraction. It stays specific. That's what makes it land. Vague longing is forgettable. But a face — a real face, with particular features — that stays. That's a person. And the poem makes no attempt to universalize it into something prettier. These particular things are stuck. And I can't unstick them.

Then the moth-to-flame arrives. And it's not romantic. Moths don't choose the flame. They're wired toward light. No deliberation, no weighing of outcomes. Just pull. Pure and indifferent to consequence.

[PAUSE]

When love starts feeling like physics instead of a decision, you're somewhere new. And the unsettling part isn't losing control — it's discovering you're not entirely unhappy about it.

The poem's real subject arrives near the end. Their ecosystems have collided.

Earlier, mind and heart worked in sync. That's the quiet operating system most of us rely on — desire checked by reason, want weighed against what makes sense. Orderly. Predictable. Safe.

Then someone walks in and the whole architecture resets.

No ground to stand on. No sky to cover my head with.

[PAUSE]

That's not chaos. It's not obsession. It's the sudden, disorienting absence of coordinates. The tools you've always used to navigate your own interior — they no longer apply. You're not broken. You're standing at a fault line where two tectonic plates have met, and the ground that was always beneath you has dissolved mid-step.

[PERSONAL_INSERT: I've had nights where one side of me was building airtight arguments for distance — rationalizing, analyzing patterns, trying to reduce emotion into something solvable like bad code. And then another part of me would still reopen old messages, still search for meaning in silence, still stand emotionally in places my logic had already left hours earlier. That's the collision the poem talks about. Not heartbreak exactly. More like realizing the mind and heart operate on completely different programming languages when something — or someone — matters enough.
]

The poem doesn't resolve this. It doesn't offer a path back to equilibrium. It just tells the truth: this is where you are now. Suspended. Recalibrating without asking to.

[BROLL: fault line in dry cracked earth, close or aerial, golden hour]

---

The heart wants what the heart wants. We say it like a disclaimer. Half surrender, half apology.

This poem says it differently. Not as an excuse. As a report from inside the collision.

Maybe when the mind and heart stop cooperating — when you're left without ground or sky — that's not a malfunction. That's a signal. The only honest one you'll get that something actually matters enough to overturn the whole system.

No coordinates. No stability. Just the full, unfiltered weight of being alive enough to feel it.

[PAUSE]

That's not a problem to solve. That's the experience itself.
