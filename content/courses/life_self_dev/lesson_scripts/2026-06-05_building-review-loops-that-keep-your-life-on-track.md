## COLD OPEN

The most expensive thing you can do with your life is run it on autopilot and check in once a year.

---

## WHY THIS MATTERS

You know that feeling when October hits and you look back at the year and think: *what happened?*

You had plans. You had goals. You wrote them down somewhere — maybe in Notes, maybe in a journal you bought on January 1st. And then life happened, which is another way of saying: you got busy, you reacted, and you stopped checking in.

This is not a discipline problem. Nobody tells you this explicitly, but most people operate this way — they do, they hope, and then they're surprised when the year didn't go where they wanted.

Here's what I've come to believe after years of doing this as a data scientist: the problem isn't the plan. It's the absence of a feedback loop.

In any system — a machine, a body, a career — things drift without feedback. A small error compounds. A wrong assumption never gets corrected. The drift becomes the direction.

A life without a review loop isn't discipline. It's hope. And hope is not a system.

This lesson is about building the simplest, lightest review loop that actually works — not the elaborate Sunday ritual you'll do three times and abandon, but something real. Something you'll actually keep.

## TEACH

### Key Point 1: What gets reviewed gets better — attention is the upgrade

I want to start with something that sounds obvious but only lands once you've experienced it.

The act of reviewing something — just *looking at it honestly* — is itself an upgrade.

Think about how this works in practice. You write down what you're eating when you're trying to eat better, and almost immediately you eat better. Not because you made a rule. Because you're watching. Attention changes behavior. The observation and the outcome are not separate events.

This is true of habits, of goals, of relationships, of your own energy. Whatever you review consistently, you improve. Not because you grind harder at it — but because noticing creates a feedback signal your brain can actually use.

The flip side is also true. Whatever you don't review, drifts. A habit you stopped tracking quietly disappears. A goal you stopped checking in on becomes something you say you care about but stopped acting on months ago.

This is why some people seem to make progress with almost everything they focus on — it's not superior intelligence or willpower, it's superior feedback. They've built in the check.

The review is not the result. But without it, results don't survive contact with the chaos of a real life.

### Key Point 2: Keep the cadence light — a heavy review is one you'll skip

Here is where most people get this wrong.

They design a review system that looks amazing on paper. A full hour on Sunday. Journaling prompts, a scorecard, a habit tracker, a gratitude section, a life goals check, a mood rating, a lessons-learned column. It's thorough. It's comprehensive. It's exhausting.

And then they do it twice. And then it becomes something they'll "get back to."

The problem with a heavy review is not that it's wrong. It's that the cost is higher than your mental energy on a tired Sunday. So the system that was supposed to help you fails the moment real life applies pressure — which is exactly when you most need it.

The best review is not the most thorough review. It's the one you'll actually do at 80% energy on a bad week.

Think about it this way: a five-minute check-in every week compounds into 260 check-ins a year. A sixty-minute deep review that happens four times is four check-ins. The math is not close.

Lightness is a feature, not a shortcut.

So what does light actually look like? Three to five questions, maximum. They need to be honest questions, not box-ticking questions. The difference is: box-ticking asks "did I do the things I said I'd do?" — and you either feel smug or guilty. An honest question asks "what actually happened and what does it tell me?" — and you come away with information.

I'll give you the specific questions in the Do This Now section. For now, the principle is: ruthlessly protect the lightness. The moment your review starts taking too long, you'll stop doing it. And a review you don't do doesn't exist.

### Key Point 3: Adjust, don't restart — tune the system instead of scrapping it

This one is the most important shift, and it's one most people never make.

When something isn't working — a habit, a schedule, a study routine — the instinct is to scrap it and start fresh. The new system. The better version. The one you'll definitely stick to *this time*.

I have done this more times than I want to admit. New month, clean slate, rebuilt the whole thing. Felt great on day one. Collapsed by day ten.

What a review loop teaches you — if you let it — is that most systems don't need to be replaced. They need to be *adjusted*.

There's a real difference. Replacing is about starting over. Adjusting is about learning. When you replace, you lose whatever was working in the old system. When you adjust, you keep it and fix the specific thing that wasn't.

A weekly review gives you the data to adjust. "This week I kept every habit except the evening one — the trigger isn't strong enough, let me move it earlier." That's a one-line fix. That doesn't need a new system.

"This week was a mess and I don't know what happened" — that's what happens without a review. And the default response to that feeling is usually to tear it all down and rebuild.

Most of the time, the system isn't broken. One part of the system is wrong. The review tells you which part.

This is what data scientists do, by the way. You don't throw out the model when it gets one prediction wrong. You look at what specifically failed, why, and what one adjustment brings it closer. That same thinking applied to your own life is powerful.

## DO THIS NOW

Your worksheet for this lesson is a 10-minute weekly review template — the actual one I use, adapted for where you are right now.

Here's what it asks. Five questions. This is the whole thing.

One: What happened this week that I'm glad happened?  
Two: What felt off or unfinished?  
Three: Did I show up the way I wanted to — in my habits, my focus, my relationships?  
Four: What's one thing I'd do differently next week?  
Five: What am I carrying into next week that I need to set down?

That's it. No scoring system. No rating out of ten. No elaborate category breakdown.

You run this at the end of the week — ideally the same time, same spot, every week. Sunday evening is common. Friday afternoon works if that's when you wrap. The consistency of *when* matters more than which time you pick.

Your worksheet has space for all five questions, plus a line at the bottom to name one adjustment you're making — not a new plan, just a one-line tune.

Your task this week: run this review once, for the first time. That's it. Not every week from now on forever — just this week. See what comes up. Most people are surprised by the fifth question.
