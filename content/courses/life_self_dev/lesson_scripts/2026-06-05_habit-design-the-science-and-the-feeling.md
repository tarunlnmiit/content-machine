## WHY THIS MATTERS

Here's what no one tells you when you're 18 and full of ambition: the habits you build — or don't build — in this decade will compound in ways you cannot fully imagine.

I'm not saying this to pressure you. I'm saying it because I lived the other version. I spent years being the person who started things, got excited, lost momentum, felt guilty, and started again. Not because I lacked desire. Because I didn't understand how habits actually work.

The difference between the person who exercises consistently and the one who doesn't isn't willpower. It's usually the location of their running shoes.

That's not a metaphor. That's the science. And once you understand it, you stop blaming yourself and start building smarter.

## TEACH

### Key Point 1: Every Habit Is a Loop You Can Build on Purpose

In the 1990s, researchers at MIT were studying rats in a maze. They found something fascinating. When a rat first navigates a new maze, its brain is highly active — processing every turn, every decision. But after it runs the same maze hundreds of times, the brain activity drops dramatically. The rat stops thinking. It just runs.

That's what a habit is. It's your brain outsourcing a behaviour to autopilot so it can focus processing power elsewhere.

And every habit — every single one — runs on a three-part loop: cue, routine, reward.

The cue is the trigger. It tells your brain: *it's time for this behaviour now.* Could be a time of day, a location, an emotion, another behaviour, or a person.

The routine is the behaviour itself. What you actually do.

The reward is what the brain gets out of it. Satisfaction, relief, pleasure — something that makes the brain say: *that was worth doing again*.

Here's what this means for you: you're not building habits from scratch. You're designing loops. If your habit isn't sticking, one of the three pieces is broken.

Most people focus entirely on the routine — *I need to exercise, I need to read, I need to meditate.* But they ignore the cue — when exactly does this behaviour start? And they ignore the reward — is my brain actually feeling good after this? Or just guilty that it wasn't better?

Build the loop first. The behaviour lives inside it.

### Key Point 2: Make It Embarrassingly Small

I want you to think about the last habit you tried to build. Now tell me: was it a reasonable amount to start with, or was it the full version you imagined doing once you were consistent?

Most people start with the full version. "I'll meditate for 20 minutes every morning." "I'll write 1,000 words every day." "I'll go to the gym for an hour five times a week."

These are not habit targets. These are aspirations wearing the costume of habits.

Stanford researcher BJ Fogg spent years studying behaviour change and arrived at something that sounds almost insulting: the smaller the habit, the more likely it is to survive.

Not because small things are more valuable. But because small things don't require motivation to start. And motivation is unreliable. It shows up when you feel good and disappears exactly when you need it most.

The rule I use: if your habit feels embarrassingly easy, you're probably in the right range.

Two push-ups. One paragraph. Three deep breaths. Open the book. Put on the shoes.

The goal isn't the two push-ups. The goal is to become the person who shows up when they said they would. The reps of showing up build the identity. The identity builds the behaviour. The behaviour — over time — builds the life.

Don't scale the habit yet. Scale after it's boring.

### Key Point 3: Design the Environment — Willpower Loses, Surroundings Win

Let me tell you the honest truth about willpower: it's a limited resource, it depletes through the day, and you are regularly in competition with people who design environments specifically to drain it from you.

Your phone is built by some of the smartest engineers on the planet to be impossible to put down. Your social feed is curated by algorithms optimised for engagement, not your wellbeing. Every notification, every autoplay, every scroll is a cue attached to a reward loop that someone else designed for you.

You cannot out-willpower a system. You have to design a better one.

James Clear — whose work on habits is some of the clearest I've encountered — puts it simply: design your environment so the right choice is also the easiest choice.

Want to read more? Put the book on your pillow. Want to eat better? Put the fruit on the counter, the snacks in the cupboard. Want to stop scrolling at midnight? Charge the phone outside your bedroom.

This isn't about punishing yourself. It's about removing the friction from the thing you want to do and adding friction to the thing you don't.

Your future self is not more disciplined. Your future self is just in a better-designed environment.

When I was trying to build a writing habit, I kept failing to find "the right time." I had good intentions, a clear goal, and I missed it almost every day. The moment I closed every other tab and put my notes document on the screen before I made my morning coffee — the writing happened. Same person. Different environment. Different result.

The cue was already there. I just made the routine impossible to avoid.
