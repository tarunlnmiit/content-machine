## COLD OPEN

You walked past something today that a poet would have stopped for.

You didn't stop. Neither did I, most days. But that's the entire difference between someone who writes poems and someone who thinks they can't — not talent, not vocabulary, not some mysterious gift. Just the habit of stopping.

That's what we're building today.

---

## WHY THIS MATTERS

Here's a problem most young writers hit fast: they have the feeling but not the poem. They know they're sad, or angry, or aching with something they can't name — and they sit down to write and what comes out is… the feeling itself. *I am so lost. Life feels meaningless. I don't know who I am.*

Which is completely true. And completely flat on the page.

The reader doesn't feel it. Because the reader hasn't lived your life. They only feel what you *show* them.

And the way you show them is through detail. Specific, physical, sensory detail. The kind that lives in the world, not in your head.

That's what observation is. Not description for its own sake. Not pretty words about sunsets. The discipline of looking at the actual thing in front of you — and trusting it to carry the weight.

---

## TEACH

**Point one: the poem lives in the detail you almost walked past.**

Not the large things. Not the dramatic things. The small ones. The ones you'd normally filter out because they seem too ordinary to matter.

A rusting window latch. The way someone stirs their tea twice and then puts the spoon down wrong. The particular silence after a door closes.

These are not background details. These *are* the poem. Or they can be, if you let them.

Here's a simple test: if you could have invented the detail, it's probably not specific enough. *A crowded street* — you invented that. *A chai stall where the owner always shortchanges by two rupees and laughs like he's surprised when you notice* — that you observed.

The observed detail has a texture that the invented one doesn't. Readers feel the difference even when they can't explain why.

So the first skill is just: *notice*. Actively. On purpose. Even when nothing seems worth noticing.

---

**Point two: concrete beats abstract.**

Let me show you what I mean with a single contrast.

Abstract: *She was grieving.*

Concrete: *She kept rearranging the objects on his desk.*

Both sentences communicate loss. But the second one — you see it. You feel the particular quality of that grief, the way it doesn't know what to do with its hands, the way it keeps returning to the same place.

The abstract tells you the category: grief. The concrete shows you the specific shape this grief took, in this body, on this afternoon.

Poetry lives in that specific shape.

Here's the practice: whenever you write an abstract emotion word — *sad, anxious, happy, lonely* — ask yourself: what did that feeling *look* like in the world? What was the body doing? What was the room doing? What small, physical thing was happening at exactly that moment?

Write that instead.

Not *sadness*. A chipped blue cup she bought alone on a trip she'd hoped would change things.

Not *joy*. The sound your shoes make on that one loose tile in the corridor that you've been stepping around for three years.

---

**Point three: the detail carries the feeling. Show the thing, not the emotion.**

This is the hardest one to trust. Because it feels like you're being indirect. Like you're hiding the point.

You're not hiding it. You're *placing* it inside something real, so the reader finds it themselves.

When a reader discovers the feeling on their own — through the image, through the detail — they feel it twice as hard. Because it's theirs now. They made the connection.

When you name the feeling directly, you rob them of that.

Think of it this way: if I tell you "this scene is sad," you're watching me respond to it. If I show you the scene — if I put the right detail in front of you — you respond to it yourself. That's the difference between reading *about* an experience and *having* one.

So the rule is: put the emotion in the object. Let the object do the work. Trust the reader to feel it.

A poem that says *I was devastated* is autobiography. A poem that shows the half-eaten dinner left on the table for three days — that's literature.

## DO THIS NOW

Before you open your worksheet, I want you to do one thing.

Spend five minutes somewhere ordinary — your room, your kitchen, outside your building. No phone. Just look.

Find one object that feels like it could carry a feeling. Don't decide what feeling. Just find the object that makes you pause.

Write three lines about only that object. No emotion words. No explanation. Just the object: what it looks like, where it is, what it does.

Then, in your worksheet, you'll find a prompt that takes this further — helping you figure out *why* that object snagged your attention, and what it might already be holding.

That's where the poem starts.

You just have to be the one who stops.
