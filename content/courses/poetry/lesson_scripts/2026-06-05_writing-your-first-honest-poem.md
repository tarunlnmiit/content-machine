## COLD OPEN

The worst poem I ever wrote had twenty lines.

The best poem I ever wrote had four.

They were the same poem.

---

## WHY THIS MATTERS

Here's what nobody tells you when you're starting: most people who say they can't write poetry are wrong. They *can* write poetry. They just keep stopping one line too early — or twenty lines too late.

You stop because you think the thing you actually want to say is too small. Too simple. Too embarrassing. So you dress it up. You add lines that sound poetic. You make it rhyme, even if the rhyme bends the truth a little.

And that bending — that's where you lose the poem.

I'm not going to teach you techniques today. I'm going to teach you three instincts. Get these right and everything else — form, structure, line breaks — becomes easier because you'll have something real to work with.

---

## TEACH

### One: Truth over rhyme

When you force a rhyme, you are choosing sound over meaning. Sometimes those two things align. Often they don't.

Here's how you know you've bent the truth for a rhyme: read the line out loud and ask, is this *exactly* what I mean? Or did I choose this word because it sounded right at the end?

Take this:

> *I held her hand beneath the rain*
> *And felt for once no trace of pain*

Maybe that's true. But maybe what you actually felt was — complicated. Relieved but guilty. Warm but already bracing for it to end.

If "no trace of pain" is not exactly right, the rhyme has stolen your poem.

The fix is not to abandon sound altogether. It's to find the honest line *first*, then see if sound serves it. Not the other way around.

Write the true thing. Then decide if it also wants to rhyme.

### Two: Cut to the real line

Every poem has one line that does the real work. One line that is the reason you sat down to write in the first place.

Most beginners hide that line. They bury it in stanza three, surrounded by safer lines — lines that explain, set up, hedge.

Here is the exercise: write the full draft, then read it back and ask — which line would I be embarrassed to read to someone? Which line makes me want to look away?

That line. That's the one.

Everything else in the poem exists to earn that line or to let it land. If a line isn't doing one of those two jobs, cut it.

This sounds simple. It is not simple. It requires you to trust that the real line is enough — that you don't need to explain it, protect it, or apologize for it.

You don't.

### Three: Short is fine

Four honest lines beat forty padded ones. Always.

The padding usually comes from not trusting the real line. So you add context. You add transitions. You add lines that gesture at what you mean because saying it plainly feels too exposed.

Here's a test: after you've written your draft, cut the first two lines. Read what remains. If it still makes sense — if it still holds — those first two lines were warm-up. The poem started without you.

Then cut the last two lines. Read it again. If it still lands — if it feels complete — you were over-explaining. The poem ended before you let it.

What's left after both cuts? That might be the poem.

## DO THIS NOW

Open your worksheet. There's a prompt at the top — one sentence, something true that happened to you this week. Something small is fine. Smaller is better.

Write it in as many lines as you need. Don't edit yet.

Then do the two cuts I described — first two lines, last two lines. Read what's left.

If it still holds — if it still feels like something — you just wrote your first honest poem.
