## COLD OPEN

The worst poem I ever wrote was also the most technically correct.

Every line broke at the right place. The imagery was layered. The final couplet rhymed — perfectly. And when I read it back, I had no idea who wrote it. It could have been anyone. It sounded like poetry. It didn't sound like me.

That's the trap. And most people who try to write poetry fall straight into it.

---

## WHY THIS MATTERS

Here's what I've noticed — and this is especially true if you grew up reading poems in school, in textbooks, in CBSE Hindi or English literature classes.

You learned what poems are *supposed* to sound like before you learned what you sound like.

You were taught that poetry has a particular gravity to it. A particular weight. A vocabulary. "Thee" and "thou" and "lo, the sun descends." Or the modern equivalent — fragmented lines, clever line breaks, vague references to the ocean as a metaphor for grief.

So when you sit down to write, the first voice that shows up isn't yours. It's a composite of every poem that ever got a ten out of ten.

And the thing is — that voice is good at a lot of things. It sounds credible. It sounds like it belongs. But it will never say the thing only you can say.

That's what this lesson is about. Not finding some mystical poetic self. Not becoming original in some grand artistic sense. Just: writing something that, if someone read it without your name on it, they would still somehow recognize as you.

---

## TEACH

**Key Point 1: Write how you actually talk, not how poems sound**

The single most effective thing you can do right now is to take your natural speaking voice and put it on the page. Unedited. Unpolished. Unpoetic.

Think about how you explain something to a friend. The rhythm of it. Where you pause. Where you speed up. The half-sentences. The specific details — not "the car" but "that old Maruti that made the sound when it turned left." Not "she was sad" but "she kept folding and unfolding the same piece of paper for twenty minutes."

That specificity, that rhythm — that's already a voice. You don't need to transform it into something. You need to trust it.

Here's a test. Write a poem. Then read it out loud. If you catch yourself slipping into a different accent — a slower, more deliberate delivery than how you normally speak — the poem isn't in your voice yet. Your voice has a pace. Your voice has particular words it loves and particular words it avoids. Your poem should have that too.

**Key Point 2: Steal structure, not soul**

This one's going to feel slightly controversial, so stay with me.

You can absolutely borrow from poets you love. You can use the shape of a poem — the form, the structure, the number of beats — without stealing the feeling.

Think of it like this. If you love the way Pablo Neruda writes a love poem, you can notice: okay, he starts with a concrete object, then moves to a larger feeling, then ends with a question. That's a structure. A template. Use it.

What you cannot do is borrow his imagery. His metaphors. The way he sees. Because when you do that, you're not finding your voice — you're wearing his.

The test is simple. After you've borrowed a form, swap out every image and ask: does this come from my life, my city, my specific experience — or does it feel borrowed?

Neruda's ocean is his. Your crowded metro platform at 8am, the smell of the canteen, the way your city looks from a bus window in the monsoon — that's yours. Same structure. Completely different poem.

**Key Point 3: The goal isn't impressive, it's unmistakably yours**

This is maybe the hardest thing to accept: impressive and authentic are often in direct conflict.

Impressive is about the reader. It's about impressing someone else, earning their approval, having them nod and think — yes, this person can write.

Authentic is about the poem. It's about accuracy. Did I say the true thing? Did I say it in the way I actually experience it?

When you're aiming for impressive, you reach for elevated vocabulary. For sweeping statements. For universal emotions described in a way that no one could disagree with.

When you're aiming for accurate, you get smaller. More specific. You write about a specific Sunday afternoon, not Sunday afternoons in general. You write about the particular quality of silence after a specific argument, not silence.

Here's the paradox: the more specific and personal you are, the more universal the poem actually becomes. Because readers don't connect to general truths. They connect to specific truths that remind them of their own specific experience.

The poem that sounds most like you will land deepest in someone else.

## DO THIS NOW

Open your worksheet for this lesson. There's one exercise on it: the Substitution Draft.

Here's how it works. Take a poem you love — any poem. Copy the structure of it: the number of lines, where it breaks, roughly how long each line is. But replace every single image, every noun, every reference with something from your own life. Your own city. Your own body. Your own specific memory.

Don't try to make it beautiful. Don't try to match the original's power. Just try to make it true.

The goal of this exercise isn't to produce a finished poem. The goal is to feel what it's like to inhabit a form that works, while the content is completely yours. You'll start to notice: this is my rhythm. This is my vocabulary. This is what I reach for when I'm not trying to reach for something impressive.

Do the full substitution. Then read both versions out loud. Notice what shifted.

That shift is the direction of your voice.
