## COLD OPEN

You wrote something. It's sitting in your notes app or your journal or a crumpled page, and you haven't shown it to a single person. Maybe you've been telling yourself it's not ready yet. Maybe you've been telling yourself you'll share it "when it's better."

It's been three months.

---

## WHY THIS MATTERS

Here's what I want you to sit with for a second. You learned a skill in this course. You practiced observation. You worked on honesty. You wrote things that actually mean something to you — maybe for the first time.

And if that writing stays only with you, forever, then what was it for?

I'm not saying you *have* to share. I'm saying most of you *want* to share but something is stopping you. And I want to name that thing, because naming it is the only way to stop letting it run the show.

---

## TEACH

### Point One: Fear is normal — and it doesn't go away

Every writer you've ever read felt this. Every poet whose work moved you — they had a moment, probably many moments, where they thought: *this is too personal, this is too weird, what will they think of me.*

The fear doesn't mean you're doing it wrong. It means you wrote something real. If it felt completely safe to share, it probably wasn't worth much.

This is important: waiting for the fear to disappear before you share is like waiting to feel confident before you try something new. The confidence comes *after*, not before. You share while afraid. That's the whole move.

I'm not going to tell you the fear goes away with practice. For most writers it doesn't, not completely. What changes is your relationship to it. You start to recognize it as a signal — *this matters to me* — instead of a warning.

---

### Point Two: Share to one person first

Not a post. Not a story. Not a group chat. *One person.*

And not just anyone. Someone who is safe. Safe doesn't mean they'll love everything you write — that's not a reader, that's a mirror that only shows you what you want to see. Safe means: this person cares about you as a person first, and their reaction won't destroy your week.

Maybe that's a close friend. Maybe it's a sibling. Maybe it's someone from this course who's been going through the same thing.

Here's the reason this works: one person is manageable. A crowd is a verdict. One person is a conversation. When you share with one person, you can ask questions. You can say *what did this make you feel?* You can sit with their response without it feeling like the world is watching.

### Point Three: A reaction is information, not a sentence on your worth

This is the one that takes the longest to actually believe, so let me say it clearly.

When someone reads your poem and says *I didn't really get it* — that is a data point. It tells you something about how the poem landed for that one reader, on that one day, with whatever they were carrying into the read. It does not tell you whether you are a writer. It does not tell you whether you should keep going. It does not tell you what you're worth.

We collapse these things together because we're human and because we put ourselves into the work. That's exactly what makes poetry powerful — and exactly why feedback feels so exposing.

Here's a shift that helped me: start asking *what did you feel?* instead of *did you like it?* Feelings are real and specific. "Like" is a judgment. If someone says *this made me feel kind of lonely and I don't know why* — that's extraordinary feedback. That tells you the poem did something. Now you can ask what, and learn from it.
