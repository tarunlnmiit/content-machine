## COLD OPEN

The hardest poem you'll ever write is the one you almost didn't start.

Not because it was difficult. Because you told yourself you weren't in the mood. And you believed it.

---

## WHY THIS MATTERS

Here's the thing about inspiration — it's not a faucet you can turn on. It's more like a well. And wells don't fill from wishing. They fill from rain, slowly, over time, whether you're watching or not.

If you're 17 and you've just discovered that you can write, that feeling is electric. But the students I've seen lose that feeling — they lose it the same way every time. They wait too long between poems. Life gets loud. Exams happen. They tell themselves they'll write when things settle. And then one day they look up and it's been four months and the pen feels foreign in their hand.

That gap is what this lesson is about. Not inspiration. Not craft. Just — how do you keep going when going feels impossible?

---

## TEACH

### Point One: Beat the blank page with a rule so small it can't lose

You don't need to write a great poem today. You need to write *a* poem today.

There's a difference.

The blank page is intimidating because we approach it with ambition. We want to say something true, something beautiful, something that will matter. And all of that ambition turns into paralysis before the first word arrives.

So remove the ambition. Replace it with a rule.

My rule was this: five minutes and one prompt. That's it. Not five minutes of *good* writing — five minutes of *any* writing. The prompt can be one word — "window," "guilt," "3am" — and your only job is to respond to it, in any direction, for five minutes.

You will write bad lines. You will write obvious lines. You will write lines that embarrass you. That's not failure — that's the cost of admission.

The blank page isn't a test. It's a door. And this rule is the key that opens it every single time, even on the worst days.

### Point Two: Small rituals outlast large bursts

Let me ask you something. What happens to a fire if you feed it only once a week?

It goes out. Doesn't matter how big the log was.

Inspiration works the same way. A three-hour writing session once a month is almost useless compared to ten minutes every day. Not because volume matters — it doesn't. Because *continuity* matters.

When you write every day, even badly, you keep the channel open. You stay in conversation with the part of you that notices things, feels things, reaches for language to hold those things. That channel, once closed, takes weeks to reopen.

So make the ritual small enough that you can't say no to it. Five minutes. A single sentence. One image in your notes app before bed.

The poem you're proud of doesn't usually come from the big dramatic writing session you planned. It comes from a Tuesday afternoon when you sat down with no expectations and something happened. And then another Tuesday. And then another.

You can't manufacture that Tuesday. But you can show up for it.

### Point Three: Know where to go from here

This course ends today. Your practice doesn't.

Here's what I want you to carry forward — three things.

First, keep the deck. Your prompt deck, the one you've built through these lessons. It's yours. Add to it. Cards from conversations, from songs, from arguments, from things you overheard on the bus. Let it grow messy and personal and strange.

Second, keep the share. Find one person — one human, not an algorithm — who you send poems to. Not for feedback. Not for validation. Just to keep the act of sharing alive. Sharing changes writing. It makes it feel like it matters, because it does.

Third, keep showing up. Not when you feel like a poet. Not when the words come easily. Show up on the ordinary days, the empty days, the days where nothing happened and nothing feels worth writing about. Those are the days that make you a writer.

A poet isn't someone who writes beautiful things. A poet is someone who keeps writing.

## DO THIS NOW

Open your worksheet for this lesson. There's one prompt at the top — a single word.

Set a timer for five minutes.

Write. Don't edit. Don't read back. Just write until the timer stops.

Then do one more thing: write down the time and place where you'll do this again tomorrow. Not "when I have time." A specific time. A specific place. That's your ritual.

If you do that twice — today and tomorrow — you've already beaten the biggest obstacle. You've proved to yourself that the blank page opens.
