## COLD OPEN

There's a moment most people never talk about — when something happens to you, something real, and you have absolutely no idea what to put it. You feel it. It's sitting right there in your chest. But the second you try to say it out loud, it just... disappears into words that don't fit.

"I'm fine."

"It's complicated."

"I don't know."

That's not a you problem. That's a language problem. And poetry — the scrappy, no-rules version of it — is the only tool I've found that actually solves it.

---

## WHY THIS MATTERS

Here's what I want you to sit with for a second.

You're probably carrying things right now that you haven't said to anyone. Maybe you can't. Maybe the person you'd tell is exactly who you're carrying things *about*. Maybe saying it out loud feels like too much, or too dramatic, or too self-indulgent when other people have bigger problems.

I know this because I was there. Because I spent years being very, very good at making sense of other people's data and having absolutely no language for my own life.

And I want to tell you something that took me embarrassingly long to learn:

The feelings don't go away because you don't name them. They just get heavier.

## TEACH

### Key Point 1: Naming a feeling changes it

There's something that happens in the brain when you move a feeling from *experience* to *language*. Scientists call it affect labeling — when you put words to what you're feeling, the part of your brain that's reacting starts to settle. Not because the problem is solved. Because you've made contact with it.

The page is where that happens for a lot of us.

When you write "I'm angry," nothing changes. But when you write — "There's this specific kind of tired that happens when someone you trusted needed you to be smaller and you did it anyway" — something shifts.

You've just said something true. And true things, once said, are lighter than they were before.

You don't have to share it. You don't have to make it good. The naming is the point.

### Key Point 2: You don't need permission, training, or talent to start

At some point, someone in your life drew a very firm line between people who write and people who don't. Maybe it was a grade on an essay. Maybe it was someone reading something you made and saying "that's nice" in the voice people use for children's drawings. Maybe it was just watching other people do it and deciding they had something you didn't.

I want to dismantle that line completely.

Poetry is not a credential. It's not a genre with rules you have to follow. It's not something that belongs to people who did well in English class or went to the right college or read the right books.

Poetry is what happens when you're honest about your experience and you let the shape of the sentence match the shape of the feeling.

That's it. That's all it is.

A 19-year-old who has never taken a single writing class can do that. A person who hates literature can do that. *You* can do that. Right now. Without asking anyone's permission.

The gatekeeping around poetry is fake. The only thing between you and it is the decision to start.

---

### Key Point 3: Start ugly — the honest mess beats the polished nothing

Here's the thing nobody tells you about the first draft: it's supposed to be bad.

Not bad in a "you'll fix it later" way. Bad in a "this is the raw material, and raw material is never finished" way. The point of the first draft is not quality. The point is getting it out of your head and onto the page where you can actually look at it.

I've seen more people get stuck waiting for the right words than I've seen people get stuck from writing the wrong ones. Because you can fix wrong words. You cannot fix a blank page.

So when you sit down to write your first poem — and you will, today, by the end of this lesson — I don't want you aiming for beautiful. I want you aiming for honest. I want you writing the thing that's been sitting in your chest and refusing to fit into polite conversation.

If it's messy, good. Messy means you found something real. Polish it later, or never. But get it out.

## DO THIS NOW

Your worksheet for this lesson has one exercise. Just one.

Set a timer for seven minutes. Write about something you haven't been able to say to anyone. It doesn't have to rhyme. It doesn't have to have a title. It doesn't have to make sense from the outside.

Write until the timer goes off. Don't stop to edit. Don't reread until you're done.

When you're finished, look at the last line. Just the last line. Ask yourself: is that true?

If it is — you just wrote a poem.

## CLOSE

The first honest thing you write will probably be terrible. Write it anyway. That's the whole practice.
