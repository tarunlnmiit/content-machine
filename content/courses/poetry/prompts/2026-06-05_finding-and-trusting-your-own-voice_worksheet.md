# Finding and Trusting Your Own Voice — Worksheet

**Objective:** By the end of this worksheet, you will have rewritten a poem entirely in your own words — no borrowed phrasing, no imitation.

---

## 1. Warm-Up: What You Actually Notice

Before you write anything poetic, you need to know what *you* pay attention to.

Spend 3 minutes looking at one ordinary thing near you right now — a ceiling fan, your water bottle, the pattern on your bedsheet, the noise outside.

Write 5 observations. Not metaphors. Not feelings. Just what you notice:

1. ____
2. ____
3. ____
4. ____
5. ____

Now circle the one that surprises you most. That is your entry point.

## 3. The Fifteen-Word Rule

Take your rewritten line. Expand it into exactly 15 words. No more, no fewer. This forces you to make every word a choice.

____

Read it aloud. If you stumble on a word — if it feels like a costume — swap it.

## 4. The Embarrassing Truth Prompt

Your voice gets loudest when you say something you were slightly afraid to say.

Answer this honestly (no one is grading the content, only whether you were honest with yourself):

*What is one thing about your daily life that you find beautiful but would sound embarrassing to admit out loud?*

## 6. Constraint Exercise — The Swap Test

Read your poem aloud, then swap out the three words you are most proud of. Replace each with a more ordinary, plainer version.

Now read the version with replacements. Does the poem collapse — or does it survive?

*If it collapses, the words were doing real work. Put them back.*
*If it survives fine, the original words were decoration. Leave the plainer ones.*
