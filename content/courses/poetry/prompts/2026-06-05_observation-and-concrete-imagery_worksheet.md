# Observation and Concrete Imagery — Worksheet

**Objective:** By the end of this worksheet, you will have captured one real scene using five concrete, specific images — the building blocks of a finished short poem.

---

## Before You Begin

Pick one ordinary scene you can physically see right now, or one you witnessed today. It doesn't have to be beautiful or dramatic. A chai stall. A ceiling fan. Your mother's hands. A crowded bus stop.

**Write your scene in one plain sentence:**

Hold this scene in mind throughout all the tasks below.

## Task 1 — Look Again (Slower This Time)

You already described the scene in one sentence. Now slow down.

List **five things you can physically see** in that scene — not feelings, not meanings, just objects and details. Be as specific as a photograph would be.

> Bad example: *a busy street*
> Good example: *a man in a yellow raincoat eating a vada pav, leaning against a blue hoarding*

## Task 2 — Bring in the Other Senses

Go back to the same scene. Add **one detail for each sense below.** Skip any sense that genuinely has nothing — don't invent.

**Sound:** ____

**Smell:** ____

**Touch or texture (something you'd feel if you reached out):** ____

**Taste (only if it exists in the scene — don't force it):** ____

---

## Task 3 — Find the Odd Detail

Look at everything you wrote in Tasks 1 and 2. Circle or mark the **one detail that surprises you** — the thing that doesn't fit, feels slightly wrong, or is oddly specific.

Write it here, and then write *why* it surprises you in a single sentence:

**The detail:** ____

**Why it surprises me:** ____

This is usually where the poem starts.

---

## Task 4 — Write the First Honest Line

Using the odd detail you found, write **one sentence that could be the first line of a poem.** No rhyme required. No poetic language required. Just an honest, concrete sentence that holds the detail.

Try two versions — one that names the thing directly, one that circles around it:

**Version B (indirect):**

Choose the one that feels truer. Mark it.

## Task 5 — The Constraint Exercise

**Rule:** Write a 5-line poem about your scene using **only words of two syllables or fewer.** No exceptions.

This forces you to cut abstract words (like *loneliness*, *nostalgia*, *beauty*) and stay in the concrete. Simple words placed carefully are almost always stronger than complicated ones.
