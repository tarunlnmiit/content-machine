There's a pattern I keep watching: the people who refuse to become characters are often the first ones the room turns against.

I published a poem this week. Not a motivational one — something colder. It came from watching authenticity get punished while performance gets rewarded, and finally deciding to name it directly.

The honest ones aren't naive. They simply care more about substance than approval. And the system has found a thousand polished ways to make them pay for that.

What stayed with me most while writing it: the people who go after genuine faces aren't confident. They're defended. Confidence doesn't need to flatten anything. Defense does. Authenticity is an indictment of performance — you can't have both in the same room and pretend they're equal.

I've spent years writing from lived experience — essays, poetry, videos about discipline, grief, and the uncomfortable parts of growth. Some of those barely moved. Then a louder, more performative version of the same idea would spread everywhere. Not because it was truer. Because it was easier to consume.

At some point, every creator faces this decision: are you building a body of work or a costume? The costume usually grows faster. The work lasts longer.

The waking up in this poem isn't despair. It's the end of being fooled. That's enough to begin with.
