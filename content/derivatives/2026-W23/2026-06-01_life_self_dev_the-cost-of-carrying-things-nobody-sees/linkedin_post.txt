We've built entire industries around measuring human performance. We have almost no language for what people carry between the metrics.

There's a kind of exhaustion that has nothing to do with how much you're doing.

It comes from what you're holding. The grief without a name. The argument that ended without closure. The version of something that happened that only you witnessed.

In psychology, unresolved emotional weight triggers what's called the Zeigarnik effect — the mind holds incomplete things the same way it holds an unfinished task. And your brain doesn't distinguish between a work problem and an emotional one. Both draw from the same finite cognitive pool.

Which explains the fog. The decisions that feel heavier than they should. The forty minutes that pass with almost nothing to show.

I've watched this play out in myself and in people I've worked alongside. Someone carries something heavy long enough and they start to seem adjacent to their own life — present but not quite there. The work still gets done. But something is running in the background.

The invisible load doesn't stay invisible. It leaks into relationships, into attention, into the quality of presence you can offer the people around you.

The first move isn't resolution. It isn't therapy or a five-step framework. It's simpler and harder than that: stop calling it nothing.
