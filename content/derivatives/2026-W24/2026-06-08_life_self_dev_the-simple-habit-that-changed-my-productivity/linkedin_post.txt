The most productive people I know don't have complicated systems — they have a handful of small habits they actually do.

I spent years chasing the perfect productivity setup. Second brain, time-block manifesto, weekly reviews with five-step sub-reviews. I was busy. I was organized. I was exhausted. And I wasn't really moving.

What changed things wasn't a new system. It was stripping back to habits simple enough to do on bad days — especially on bad days. Seven of them.

The highest-impact one isn't a morning routine. It's how you end the day. Most people just stop working and carry the open threads into dinner, into family time, into sleep. A four-minute shutdown ritual — scan what's unfinished, note where you'll pick it up, write one line on what got done — gives the nervous system permission to stop tracking. Better sleep is one result. The bigger one is sitting down the next morning already knowing where to begin.

The pattern across all seven habits isn't output maximization. It's contact — with how you're spending attention, with what you actually did, with what matters this week. Systems fail when life gets imperfect. These habits ask for honest, not perfect.

You don't need all seven. Start with the one that stings a little when you read it.
