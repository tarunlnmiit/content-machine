I spent 3 years building complex productivity systems. Then I stripped back to 7 habits. Here's what they are:

1/ The shutdown ritual. Four minutes at day's end: scan tasks, note what's unfinished, write one line on what got done. Then say out loud — literally out loud — 'Shutdown complete.' Your brain can't stop tracking open loops until you give it a signal that work is over.

2/ The weekly brain dump. Every Sunday, 20 minutes. Write everything in your head — tasks, worries, half-formed ideas, things you promised people. Not organized. Not prioritized. Just out. Working memory is small. Stop making it do the work of storage.

3/ Eat the frog — but only ONE. I used to write three frogs. Then five. The power isn't in doing hard things early. It's in ending the cognitive drag of avoidance. One frog kills one background program. Multiple frogs multiplies the drag, not the output.

4/ The done list. At day's end, write the 5 most meaningful things you did — not everything, just what moved something. To-do lists measure what's left. Done lists measure what happened. Over time, this corrects how you estimate your own capacity.

5/ One 90-minute deep work block. Not four 25-minute Pomodoros. Every context switch costs 15–20 minutes of recovery. Short blocks end right as your brain hits its stride. One long block produces more than a fractured day of task-switching.

6/ Write tomorrow's top 3 tonight. Three forces a choice. Five doesn't. The morning version of you should never decide what to work on — that decision should already be made, by you, the night before, with full context.

7/ Track one metric per week. One number. Whatever matters most right now. What gets measured gets noticed. Noticing, repeated across weeks, builds the self-awareness that good work requires.

None of these need an app. They need a decision on bad days.
