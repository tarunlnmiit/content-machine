Nobody mocks you for hiring a personal trainer. Mention a therapist and suddenly you're 'not strong enough to handle your own life.' Same logic. Completely different social response.

I started therapy from exhaustion, not clarity. I'd been passive-aggressive in too many rooms, damaged too many interactions I actually cared about, burned through too many sleepless nights to keep telling myself I had it handled. The logic of self-sufficiency was costing more than it was protecting.

First session, my therapist asked: 'What would you say to a friend going through what you're going through?' The answer came fast. You don't have to carry this alone. You deserve support.

Then: why wouldn't I say that to myself?

That question cracked something open. Not fixed — cracked open. Which is how most real progress starts.

Research by Priory Group found that 40% of men won't talk about their mental health to anyone. Globally, men die by suicide at significantly higher rates than women. These aren't abstract statistics. They're the downstream cost of a generation of 'I'm fine.'

Therapy is a tool for growth — not evidence that you've failed, but evidence that you're taking your life seriously enough to want to do better at it.

The strength we inherited is worth questioning. When did you stop believing that asking for help was weakness?
