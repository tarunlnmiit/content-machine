Break a bone, people sign your cast. Tell them you haven't slept in weeks because your chest is caving in — and the room goes quiet. The strength we inherited is broken:

40% of men won't talk about their mental health to anyone. Not friends. Not family. Not anyone. Globally, men die by suicide at significantly higher rates than women. That's not a personality quirk. That's the cost of inherited silence.

The stigma is furniture nobody uses but nobody throws away. 'Real men don't cry.' 'Therapy is for people who can't handle life.' These scripts ran so long they became wallpaper. Nobody notices them anymore — but they're still running.

A friend called me late once. Flat voice. Long pauses. I could hear it immediately. What did I do? Went into optimization mode. Budgeting tools. Side income ideas. Resume tweaks. He said quietly: 'Yeah... I know all that.' He hadn't called for solutions. He called to not be alone.

Nobody mocks you for hiring a personal trainer. No stigma in wanting to get stronger and needing expertise. But mention a therapist — suddenly you're 'not strong enough to handle your life.' Same logic. Completely different social response.

I started therapy from exhaustion, not clarity. First session: 'What would you say to a friend going through what you're going through?' Answer came fast. You don't have to carry this alone. Then the obvious question: why wouldn't I say that to myself? That cracked something open.

Strength built on suppression looks solid until the right pressure hits — then it shatters. Strength built on honesty bends. It doesn't break. Vulnerability isn't the opposite of strength. It's proof of it.

The wall of silence doesn't fall at once. It gets chipped from both sides. Every time someone chooses honesty over the performance of being fine, it loosens the grip. Start there. Follow if this is the kind of writing you need.
