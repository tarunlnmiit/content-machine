There's a specific kind of disorientation that doesn't come with a warning — the moment someone occupies your mental bandwidth so completely that your own operating system starts behaving strangely.

I've been thinking about the way we describe being consumed by another person. We reach for the same metaphors — falling, drowning, burning — and most of them frame it as something happening to us.

But there's a more precise image I keep returning to: losing your coordinates.

Before, the mind and heart worked in sync. Desire got checked by reason. Want got weighed against what made sense. A decision emerged. Orderly. Predictable. Safe.

Then someone walks in and the whole architecture resets.

I was deep in a debugging session once — multiple terminals open, coffee going cold beside me — and a sentence from a conversation days earlier replayed with cinematic clarity. Not the important parts. Just the way she laughed halfway through it. The mind protects what it believes matters, even when you'd prefer otherwise.

What strikes me about genuinely powerful poetry is that it doesn't try to resolve this tension. It names the location accurately.

No ground to stand on. No sky to cover your head with.

Not a malfunction. Not weakness. A fault line. The only honest signal that something actually matters enough to overturn the system.
