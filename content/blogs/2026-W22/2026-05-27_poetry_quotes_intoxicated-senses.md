## HOOK

There's a specific kind of madness that doesn't announce itself. One day you're fine. The next, someone's laugh is living rent-free in your head, your morning coffee tastes different, and the part of your brain that's supposed to run the show has quietly handed over the keys.

The trouble isn't the feeling. It's what comes after — when you realize you've been running on a hangover you didn't consent to. And it refuses to lift.

---

## POEM

> Love has taken away
> all my senses
> like that of alcohol
> effects of booze reside
> for a short period
> but your intoxication
> doesn't want to go away
> my heart keeps enjoying the hangover you created
> your twinkling eyes
> your enlightening smile
> your ebony hair
> are stuck with me
> like a newborn sticks to her mother
> my mind finds no way
> to get around you
> it keeps coming back
> like a moth attracted to the flame
> like waves to the shore
> like cold to the winter
> like a rainbow after the rain
> I know
> this isn't going to do any good to me
> but the heart wants what the heart wants
> earlier
> my mind and heart worked in sync
> but it seems
> like their ecosystems have collided
> deciding not to comply with each other
> leaving me without a ground to stand on
> or a sky to cover my head with

---

## REFLECTION 1

The hangover metaphor works precisely because it has an endpoint — and this doesn't.

Alcohol leaves. You sleep it off, you hydrate, you move on. But this intoxication doesn't schedule a checkout time. There's no next morning where the clarity returns clean. The twinkling eyes, the enlightening smile, the ebony hair — they don't fade. They stay specific. Vivid. Named.

That specificity is everything. Vague longing is forgettable. But *ebony hair*, an *enlightening smile* — that's a face. That's a person. A real one. And the poem doesn't try to universalize it into something prettier. It stays honest: these particular things are stuck, and I can't unstick them.

Then the moth-to-flame arrives — and it doesn't romanticize. Moths don't choose the flame. They're wired toward light. No deliberation, no weighing of outcomes. Just pull. Pure and indifferent to consequence.

When love starts feeling like physics instead of a decision, you're somewhere new. And the unsettling part isn't losing control — it's discovering you're not entirely unhappy about it.

`[There’s a strange humiliation in realizing how thoroughly someone has occupied your mental bandwidth. I remember working on a machine learning workflow once — deep in debugging, multiple terminals open, coffee going cold beside me — and out of nowhere, a sentence she had said days earlier replayed in my head with cinematic clarity. Not the important parts. Not the dramatic parts. Just the way she laughed halfway through it. The kind of detail your brain should’ve discarded instantly. Instead, it stayed. That was the unsettling realization: the mind protects what it believes matters, even when you’d rather it didn’t.]`

## REFLECTION 2

The poem's real subject arrives near the end: *their ecosystems have collided*.

Earlier, mind and heart worked in sync. That's the quiet operating system most of us rely on — desire checked by reason, want weighed against what makes sense. You feel something, your mind processes it, a decision emerges. Orderly. Predictable. Safe.

Then someone walks in and the whole architecture resets.

*No ground to stand on. No sky to cover my head with.*

That's the poem's most precise image — not chaos, not obsession. Just the sudden, disorienting absence of coordinates. The tools you've always used to navigate your own interior life no longer apply. You're not broken. You're not weak. You're standing at a fault line where two tectonic plates have met, and the ground that was always beneath you has dissolved mid-step.

The poem doesn't try to resolve this. It doesn't offer a path back to equilibrium. It just tells the truth: this is where you are now. Suspended. Recalibrating without asking to.

`[I’ve had periods where logic built an airtight case for distance — focus on work, stabilize life, protect momentum, stop revisiting what clearly hurts. Rationally, the equation made sense. But the emotional side kept refusing closure like it had evidence the mind didn’t have access to yet. It’s difficult explaining that split to people who think decisions are made cleanly. Sometimes they aren’t. Sometimes one half of you is trying to move on while the other is still standing in the ruins looking for meaning. And the exhausting part isn’t choosing one side — it’s realizing both sides sound convincing when you’re alone at 2 AM.]`

---

## TAKEAWAY

*The heart wants what the heart wants.* We say it like a disclaimer — half surrender, half apology.

This poem says it differently. Not as an excuse. As a report from inside the collision.

Maybe the fault line isn't a failure. Maybe when the mind and heart stop cooperating, when you're left without ground or sky, that's not a malfunction — it's a signal. The only honest one you'll get that something actually matters enough to overturn the system.

No coordinates. No stability. Just the full, unfiltered weight of being alive enough to feel it.

That's not a problem to solve. That's the experience itself.
