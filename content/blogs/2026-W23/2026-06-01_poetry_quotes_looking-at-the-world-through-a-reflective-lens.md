# Waking Up to What We've Built

---

There's a moment — usually quiet, usually late — when the performance stops making sense. You've watched someone get torn apart for being real. You've watched the hollow ones get praised. And something in you goes still, then cold. This poem came from that moment. Not rage. Clarity.

---

> Waking up to a realization
> About how the world is changing
> Thoughts driven by exteriority
> No care about what lies within
> People drooling over aesthetics
> Shitting all over the hearts of the good ones
> Praising the false gods
> Demeaning deserving humans
> Talking about love and life
> Not knowing the true meaning
> How our civilization has turned out
> wearing layers of masks themselves
> ready to tear down the true faces
> Only to make themselves happy
> Only to make themselves happy

## What This Does to You

The line that hits hardest: *shitting all over the hearts of the good ones.*

No softening. No metaphor to hide behind. That's what it actually feels like — watching people who show up honestly get dismantled, while the ones performing correctly get lifted.

The good ones aren't naive. They're just not willing to trade their interior for applause. And the world has found a thousand polished ways to punish that.

`[PERSONAL_INSERT: I've felt this most sharply as a creator.
The internet says it wants authenticity, but the numbers often tell a different story. I've spent hours writing essays, poems, and videos that came from lived experience—pieces about discipline, relationships, grief, purpose, and the uncomfortable parts of growth. Sometimes those barely moved. Then I'd watch a louder, cleaner, more performative version of the same idea spread everywhere.
Not because it was truer. Because it was easier to consume.
There comes a point where every creator has to decide whether they're building a body of work or a costume. The costume usually grows faster. The work lasts longer.
]`

The poem names this as a civilizational thing, not just a bad week. *How our civilization has turned out.* That widening is important. This isn't individual cruelty. It's a system that rewards surface and calls it culture.

---

## The Mask Paradox

Here's what cuts deepest: the ones tearing down true faces are themselves masked.

They're not confident. They're defended. There's a difference. Confidence doesn't need to flatten anything. Defense does.

The mask doesn't just hide — it needs to. It needs the authentic face to disappear, because authenticity is an indictment of performance. You can't have both in the same room and pretend they're equal.

So the masked world doesn't just ignore genuine people. It goes after them. Specifically. Deliberately. *Ready to tear down the true faces* — not by accident, not incidentally. Ready.

`[PERSONAL_INSERT: I've watched this happen to people who refused to become characters.
Some of the most thoughtful people I've met weren't the loudest in the room. They weren't masters of personal branding. They weren't constantly optimizing themselves for approval. They simply cared more about substance than performance.
And because of that, they were often overlooked.
Meanwhile, the people who knew exactly how to package themselves kept receiving the attention, opportunities, and praise. Not always because they were better. Sometimes because they were better at being seen.
That distinction has stayed with me for years.
]`

And the repeated ending tells you everything about the motive: *Only to make themselves happy. Only to make themselves happy.* Not righteousness. Not values. Just hunger wearing borrowed language about love and life.

---

## The One Thing to Carry

The poem doesn't offer a fix. It offers a name.

Naming something doesn't solve it, but it ends a certain confusion — the confusion of thinking you're the problem when you refuse to wear the mask. You're not. You're just visible in a world that's learned to find visibility threatening.

The waking up in the first line isn't despair. It's the end of being fooled.

That's enough to begin with.

If this poem found a nerve — share it with someone who's been worn down by the performance. Not to explain it. Just to say: *I see it too.*
